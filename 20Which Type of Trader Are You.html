Which Type of Trader Are You?

Learn how to cut through the hype and choose a trading approach that fits your actual life, temperament, and risk tolerance — and identify the approach that best matches how you think, work, and make decisions.

01 Decision Framework

How to Choose a Trading Style That Actually Fits You

Stop choosing styles that flatter your ego — start choosing styles you can actually execute.

This module extends the Trader Profile built in Module 19. Each style below differs on five practical dimensions: required schedule, decision-making pace, sensitivity to trading costs, overnight risk exposure, and psychological demands. Evaluating yourself honestly against these five dimensions, before picking a style for its perceived excitement or prestige, is what actually predicts whether you'll stick with it.

DimensionWhat to ask yourself
ScheduleHow many uninterrupted hours can you realistically commit, and when?
Decision paceDo you think well quickly under pressure, or do you prefer time to deliberate?
Cost sensitivityHow much do spreads and commissions matter to your strategy's math, given your typical target size?
Overnight risk toleranceAre you comfortable holding open exposure while you sleep?
Psychological fitDo frequent small decisions energize or exhaust you?
02 Style Profile

What Is Day Trading?

Closing trades before the day ends removes one kind of risk but does NOT remove stupidity.

Day trading means opening and closing all positions within the same trading day, avoiding overnight exposure entirely. It demands real-time chart-watching during active hours, fast but not split-second decision-making, and a meaningful daily time commitment — typically several focused hours per session.

TraitDay trading
Typical holding periodMinutes to hours, closed by end of day
Screen time neededHigh — several focused hours per session
Overnight riskNone by design
Cost sensitivityModerate to high — more trades than swing/position styles
The myth this lesson corrects: avoiding overnight risk by closing trades daily does nothing to fix a flawed setup, poor risk management, or a lack of discipline — it removes one specific risk, not the need for skill.
03 Style Profile

What Is Scalping?

Most beginners should treat scalping as something to understand, NOT something to copy.

Scalping is an even faster, more intense version of day trading, targeting very small price moves over seconds to minutes, often with many trades per session. Because profit targets are so small, trading costs (spread, commission, slippage) consume a much larger share of each trade's potential profit than in slower styles — meaning execution quality and broker conditions matter enormously.

TraitScalping
Typical holding periodSeconds to minutes
Screen time neededVery high — continuous attention during sessions
Cost sensitivityExtremely high — costs are a large share of each small target
Execution demandsFast, low-latency execution and tight spreads are essential
Why this isn't a beginner-friendly starting point: the combination of high cost sensitivity, intense screen-time demands, and the need for excellent execution infrastructure makes scalping one of the least forgiving styles to learn on while also building basic skills like reading price action and managing risk.
04 Style Profile

What Is Swing Trading?

"Slower" does NOT mean "easy."

Swing trading holds positions for several days to a few weeks, aiming to capture a larger directional move than intraday styles target. This gives more breathing room than day trading, but introduces overnight and weekend risk — gaps, surprise news, and weekend geopolitical developments can all affect an open position while you're away from the screen.

TraitSwing trading
Typical holding periodSeveral days to a few weeks
Screen time neededModerate — a few dedicated check-ins daily
Overnight/weekend riskPresent and must be actively managed
Cost sensitivityLower — fewer trades, larger targets relative to costs
05 Style Profile

What Is Position Trading?

"Slow" does NOT mean "basically investing with extra swagger."

Position trading holds trades for weeks to months, built around bigger-picture themes — often blending the macro fundamental frameworks from Modules 05–18 with price action timing from Modules 01–04. Longer holding periods change risk and sizing considerations: wider stops are typically needed to accommodate normal volatility over a longer horizon, which in turn affects appropriate position size (Module 24).

TraitPosition trading
Typical holding periodWeeks to months
Screen time neededLow — periodic review rather than constant monitoring
Stop-loss widthWider, to accommodate normal volatility over a longer horizon
Analytical mixHeavier reliance on fundamentals, lighter reliance on short-term price timing
Why it still demands real skill: position trading still requires disciplined risk management, a real thesis with a defined invalidation point (Module 08), and the patience to sit through normal volatility without abandoning a sound idea prematurely.
06 Synthesis

What Type of Trader Are You?

Eliminate what obviously doesn't fit, then identify your best match and backup match.

The goal of this lesson isn't to find the "best" style in the abstract — there isn't one. It's to leave with one clear starting lane that matches your actual Trader Profile from Module 19, rather than several half-considered options that all sound appealing but don't fit your real life.

Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Trading Styles

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. Why is cost sensitivity especially critical for scalping compared to position trading?

Answer: A. With very small per-trade targets, costs eat a disproportionately large share of scalping's potential profit compared to slower styles with larger targets.

2. What risk does day trading specifically avoid by design?

Answer: A. Closing all trades within the day removes overnight exposure specifically, but doesn't eliminate the need for skill or risk management.

3. Why does position trading typically require wider stop-losses than day trading?

Answer: A. Longer holding periods involve more normal price fluctuation, requiring a wider stop to avoid being prematurely stopped out by routine volatility.

4. What is the practical goal of evaluating yourself against the five dimensions in this module (schedule, pace, cost sensitivity, overnight risk, psychological fit)?

Answer: B. There's no universally best style — the goal is finding the one that genuinely fits your real circumstances and personality.
