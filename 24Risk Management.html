Module 24 of the Trading Curriculum

Risk Management

Most traders lose money not because they can't read charts, but because they never learned how to protect what they have. The traders who last are not the ones who win the most. They are the ones who know how to lose safely.

01 Foundations

What Is Risk Management (and Why Most Traders Ignore It)

Winning more trades than you lose still isn't enough to save you.

Risk management is the set of rules governing how much you can lose on any single trade, idea, day, or period — designed so that being wrong, which happens to every trader regularly, never threatens your ability to keep trading. Most beginners skip straight to strategy (entries, indicators, setups) because that's the exciting part. Risk management feels like the boring prerequisite — right up until an oversized loss erases months of gains in a single trade.

The uncomfortable truth: a system with a high win rate and no risk discipline can still blow up. A system with a modest win rate and strict risk discipline can survive indefinitely. Survival, not win rate, is the prerequisite for long-term profitability.
02 The Core Math

The Math of Losing: How Drawdown Works and Why Your Losses Are Harder to Recover From Than You Think

The breakeven formula, and your broker's mandatory drawdown rule.

A loss and the gain required to recover from it are not symmetrical — the deeper the drawdown, the disproportionately larger the recovery gain required, because losses shrink your base capital.

DrawdownGain required to recover (on remaining capital)
10%11.1%
20%25%
30%42.9%
50%100%
75%300%
Built-in broker mechanics: leveraged accounts carry a mandatory stop-out level — a point at which the broker automatically closes positions to prevent the account from going negative. This is not a safety net you should rely on; by the time it triggers, the damage to your account is already severe.
03 The Core Rule

How Much Should You Risk Per Trade?

Why the old "2% rule" has been updated, and why your broker's leverage offer has little to do with how much you should risk.

A long-standing rule of thumb suggested risking around 1–2% of account equity per trade. Many experienced traders today favor an even more conservative range — often 0.5–1% — particularly for beginners still validating a system (Module 21), since smaller per-trade risk buys more trades' worth of room to learn before any single string of losses becomes account-threatening.

Risk per tradeConsecutive losses to lose 20% of account
0.5%~40 trades
1%~20 trades
2%~10 trades
5%~4 trades
Leverage offered ≠ risk taken. Your broker's maximum leverage (Module 25) determines how large a position you're permitted to open — it says nothing about how much you should actually risk on any single idea. Conflating the two is one of the most common and dangerous beginner mistakes.

06 Measuring Your Edge

Reward-to-Risk Ratio and Expectancy: The Two Numbers That Define Your Edge

Why your win rate alone tells you almost nothing.

This extends directly from Module 22, lesson 5. The reward-to-risk ratio (R:R) compares your planned profit target to your planned stop-loss distance — a 2:1 R:R trade aims to win twice what it risks. Combined with win rate, R:R produces expectancy, the single number that tells you whether a system is actually profitable over time.

Win rateMin. R:R needed to break even
30%~2.33:1
40%1.5:1
50%1:1
60%~0.67:1
The practical implication: a lower win rate is entirely sustainable, even desirable, if your reward-to-risk ratio comfortably clears the corresponding breakeven threshold above.
07 Synthesis

Building Your Personal Risk Management Plan

Combine everything into a single written plan — and write it down, since that's the part most traders skip.

  1. Set your per-trade risk percentage (lesson 3), matched to your risk attitude (Module 23) and system maturity (Module 21).
  2. Set your layered risk budget — per-idea, per-day, and circuit breaker limits (lesson 4).
  3. Commit to calculating position size every trade using the formula from lesson 5, never trading a habitual or "round" size.
  4. Set a minimum acceptable reward-to-risk ratio for any setup, informed by your system's actual win rate (lesson 6).
  5. Write it all down as part of your broader trading plan (Module 19) — an unwritten risk plan is far easier to silently abandon under pressure than a written one.
Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Risk Management

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. Why isn't a high win rate alone sufficient to guarantee long-term survival as a trader?

Answer: A. Without disciplined risk management, even a high win-rate system remains vulnerable to a single catastrophic loss.

2. Why is a 50% drawdown disproportionately harder to recover from than a 20% drawdown?

Answer: A. Because losses shrink the base capital you're recovering from, the required percentage gain accelerates sharply as drawdown deepens.

3. Why does the amount of leverage your broker offers NOT determine how much you should risk per trade?

Answer: A. Conflating available leverage with appropriate risk is one of the most dangerous and common beginner mistakes.

4. Why are per-trade risk limits alone insufficient to fully protect an account?

Answer: A. A layered risk budget (per-trade, per-idea, per-day, and circuit breakers) catches failure modes that a single per-trade limit can't.

5. With a 40% win rate, approximately what reward-to-risk ratio is needed just to break even?

Answer: A. At a 40% win rate, a reward-to-risk ratio of roughly 1.5:1 is the breakeven threshold — anything better is genuinely profitable over time.
