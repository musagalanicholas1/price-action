Module 05 of the Trading Curriculum

Fundamental Analysis: A Macro Fundamentals Approach

Most traders watch the charts. The ones who understand WHY currencies move watch the economies behind them. This module teaches macro fundamentals — growth, inflation, central banks, monetary policy, and fiscal policy — and how they all drive currency price action.

01 Foundations

What Is Fundamental Analysis? And Why It's Really Macro

How it differs from stock market fundamentals, and why economic indicators, central banks, and geopolitics matter.

In equity markets, fundamental analysis usually means analyzing a single company: its earnings, debt, management, and competitive position. In forex, there's no single "company" behind a currency — there's an entire economy. Fundamental analysis in forex is therefore really macroeconomic analysis: studying growth, inflation, employment, trade flows, central bank policy, and government finances for an entire country (or currency bloc, in the case of the euro), and comparing that picture against another country's.

Every currency pair is a relative bet. EUR/USD isn't a statement about the euro in isolation — it's a statement about the eurozone economy relative to the U.S. economy. This relative framing is the single most important mental habit this module will build.

The practical question fundamental analysis always answers: is the economic story behind Currency A getting relatively stronger or weaker compared to the story behind Currency B?
02 Foundations

The Economic Cycle: Where Every Fundamental Analysis Starts

The map that makes every economic data point, central bank decision, and news event make sense.

Economies move through recurring phases of expansion and contraction known as the business cycle. Before interpreting any individual data release, you need a working sense of where the broader economy currently sits in this cycle — the same data point means something very different depending on the phase.

PhaseTypical signsUsual central bank posture
ExpansionRising GDP, falling unemployment, rising consumer spendingGradually less accommodative; watching for overheating
PeakGrowth at its strongest, inflation pressures buildingOften hiking rates or signaling the end of easing
ContractionFalling GDP, rising unemployment, weaker spendingCutting rates, providing stimulus
Trough / recoveryGrowth bottoms out and begins to turn higherHolding rates low, supporting the early recovery

Knowing the phase tells you what kind of data surprises to expect and how a central bank is likely to react to them — which is the foundation for the next two lessons on growth and inflation.

03 The Two Pillars

Economic Growth: The Force That Shapes Central Bank Thinking

How to read growth before the headline numbers arrive, and why growth differentials drive the biggest currency trends.

Central banks watch two things above all else before setting policy: growth and inflation (covered next). Growth, typically measured by Gross Domestic Product (GDP), tells policymakers whether the economy needs support or restraint.

Reading Growth Before the Headline GDP Print

GDP itself is reported with a significant lag and is often revised. Traders watch faster, more frequent proxies to estimate growth before the official number arrives:

  • Purchasing Managers' Index (PMI) — a monthly survey of manufacturing and services activity; readings above 50 signal expansion, below 50 signal contraction.
  • Retail sales — a direct read on consumer spending, the largest component of GDP in most developed economies.
  • Industrial production — manufacturing output, more relevant in economies with a larger industrial base.
  • Employment data — covered in more depth in Module 06, but a leading proxy for income and spending capacity.

Why Growth Differentials Matter More Than Growth Levels

A currency doesn't need to belong to a booming economy to strengthen — it needs to belong to an economy growing faster than its counterpart. A country growing at a modest 1.5% looks weak in isolation, but if its trading partner is growing at 0.5%, the growth differential still favors the stronger currency.

04 The Two Pillars

Inflation: The Force That Moves Central Banks

How CPI, PCE, and PPI measure it, why central banks target 2%, and how inflation regimes shape currency values.

Inflation measures how fast prices are rising across the economy. Most major central banks target roughly 2% annual inflation — low enough to preserve purchasing power and price stability, high enough to avoid the dangers of deflation.

The Three Main Inflation Gauges

MeasureWhat it tracksUsed most by
CPI (Consumer Price Index)A fixed basket of consumer goods and servicesMost central banks and headline media reporting
PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures)A broader, basket-adjusting measure of consumer spendingThe U.S. Federal Reserve's preferred gauge
PPI (Producer Price Index)Prices received by producers/wholesalers — often leads CPIUsed as an early warning signal for future consumer inflation

Inflation Regimes

Why this matters for currencies: a central bank fighting overheating inflation typically hikes rates (often currency-supportive in the near term), while one facing stagflation faces an unusually difficult, currency-ambiguous policy trade-off.

How Policy Transmits Through the Economy

Monetary Policy Divergence

When two central banks are moving in opposite directions — one hiking while another cuts, or one signaling more tightening while another signals an end to its cycle — the resulting interest rate differential tends to produce some of the cleanest, most sustained trends in all of forex. This concept, "divergence," reappears constantly throughout the rest of this module and the curriculum.

06 Reading Language

Hawkish vs. Dovish: How to Read Central Bank Language

A single speech can move a currency more than the rate decision itself.

Hawkish language signals a central bank leaning toward tighter policy — higher rates, less stimulus, more concern about inflation than growth. Dovish language signals the opposite — a willingness to cut rates or maintain stimulus, more concern about growth or employment than inflation.

Reading the Shift, Not Just the Stance

What moves currencies most isn't whether a central bank is "hawkish" or "dovish" in absolute terms — it's whether they're more hawkish or dovish than the market expected. A central bank seen as dovish that turns out to be only mildly dovish can still strengthen its currency, because the surprise was a hawkish one relative to expectations. This concept (market expectations) gets its own full treatment in lesson 10.

07 The Core Driver

Interest Rates: The Force That Moves Currencies

Rate differentials, real vs. nominal rates, and the bond market's role as a real-time forecast.

Higher interest rates tend to attract foreign capital seeking better returns on deposits and bonds denominated in that currency — increasing demand for the currency itself. This is the single most consistently cited driver of medium-term currency trends.

Nominal vs. Real Interest Rates

The nominal rate is the rate you see quoted. The real rate subtracts inflation: Real Rate ≈ Nominal Rate − Inflation. A country offering a 5% nominal rate with 6% inflation has a negative real rate — savers are actually losing purchasing power. Currency markets generally care more about real rates than nominal ones, since real rates reflect the true return being offered.

The Bond Market as a Real-Time Forecast

Government bond yields move continuously and reflect the market's evolving expectations for future interest rates — often before a central bank actually acts. Rising yields in one country relative to another, ahead of any formal rate decision, are frequently an early signal that a rate differential (and the FX move that tends to follow it) is already forming.

08 The Institutions

Central Banks: The Institutions Behind Every Currency Move

The Fed, ECB, BoJ, BoE, BoC, RBA, RBNZ, SNB, and PBOC — mandates, signaling, and credibility.

Nine central banks dominate the major forex pairs. Each has a distinct mandate, decision calendar, and communication style — but all of them are ultimately trying to balance the same growth/inflation tension covered earlier in this module.

Central bankCurrencyPrimary mandate
Federal Reserve (Fed)USDDual mandate: maximum employment and price stability
European Central Bank (ECB)EURPrice stability (~2% inflation) across the eurozone
Bank of Japan (BoJ)JPYPrice stability, with a long history of fighting deflation
Bank of England (BoE)GBPInflation targeting (2%), with financial stability oversight
Bank of Canada (BoC)CADInflation targeting (2%), closely tied to commodity cycles
Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA)AUDInflation targeting alongside full employment and economic welfare
Reserve Bank of New Zealand (RBNZ)NZDInflation targeting, was the first central bank globally to formally adopt it
Swiss National Bank (SNB)CHFPrice stability, with a history of direct FX market intervention
People's Bank of China (PBOC)CNYMultiple objectives including growth, employment, and a managed exchange rate
Credibility is priced in directly. A central bank with a long track record of following through on its stated commitments gets believed faster by markets — its forward guidance moves currencies more efficiently than one with a history of reversing course unexpectedly.
09 Behavioral Patterns

Central Banks Have Personalities: Decode Their Behavior

Four traits that make each institution predictable.

Trait 1

First-mover tendency

Some central banks (historically the Fed) tend to move first in a global cycle; others tend to wait and follow.

Trait 2

Communication style

Some are highly transparent with detailed forward guidance (RBNZ, Fed); others are more deliberately ambiguous (ECB at times, BoJ historically).

Trait 3

Intervention willingness

The SNB and BoJ have both directly intervened in FX markets when they judged their currency's level a problem; others (the Fed) almost never do.

Trait 4

Inflation-success definition

Some banks treat the inflation target as a hard ceiling to avoid exceeding; others (the Fed's average-inflation-targeting framework) treat it as an average to manage over time.

Learning each bank's personality lets you anticipate how they're likely to react to the same data surprise differently — a hot inflation print might trigger swift hawkish language from one institution and a measured "we'll watch the data" response from another, purely based on institutional character.

10 Application

How to Trade Central Bank Decisions Using Market Expectations

Getting the rate decision right isn't enough if the market already expected it.

"Priced in" means the market has already adjusted asset prices to reflect the outcome it expects — so when that expected outcome actually arrives, there's little reason for the price to move further on the news alone. What actually moves the market is the surprise: the gap between what was priced in and what was delivered.

The Four Scenarios

ScenarioTypical currency reaction
Hawkish decision, hawkish surprise (more than priced in)Currency strengthens, often sharply
Hawkish decision, dovish surprise (less than priced in)Currency can weaken despite the "hawkish" headline, since it undershot expectations
Dovish decision, dovish surprise (more than priced in)Currency weakens, often sharply
Dovish decision, hawkish surprise (less than priced in)Currency can strengthen despite the "dovish" headline

On decision day, for any of the nine major central banks, the practical checklist is the same: know the market-implied probability beforehand, watch the actual decision and accompanying statement language, and react to the gap between the two — not the headline in isolation.

11 Beyond the Central Bank

Beyond the Central Bank: How Fiscal Policy Also Moves Currencies

Governments are steering the economy with a different set of controls than the central bank.

Fiscal policy refers to government decisions on spending and taxation — distinct from the monetary policy levers covered earlier in this module, which belong to the central bank. Large fiscal stimulus (spending increases or tax cuts) can boost growth and, in some conditions, inflation; fiscal tightening (austerity) does the reverse.

Why Debt Levels Matter

Persistently large government deficits, financed by rising debt, can eventually raise concerns about a country's fiscal sustainability — pushing up the yields a government must offer to borrow, and in extreme cases damaging confidence in the currency itself.

When Fiscal and Monetary Policy Conflict

When monetary and fiscal policy point in opposite directions, the net effect on a currency becomes genuinely harder to call, and usually depends on which force the market judges to be more dominant at that moment in the cycle.

Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Fundamental Analysis — A Macro Fundamentals Approach

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. Why is fundamental analysis in forex described as fundamentally "macro" rather than company-style analysis?

Answer: A. Currency pairs are relative bets between two economies, which is why forex fundamental analysis is really macroeconomic and comparative by nature.

2. Why does a country growing at a modest 1.5% sometimes still see currency strength?

Answer: B. Currency markets price relative growth differentials, not absolute growth levels in isolation.

3. A country offers a 5% nominal interest rate but has 6% inflation. What is its real rate, and why does that matter more than the nominal figure?

Answer: B. Real rate ≈ nominal rate − inflation. A negative real rate means savers are losing purchasing power despite a seemingly attractive nominal yield.

4. A central bank cuts rates (a "dovish" decision), but the market had priced in an even larger cut. What's the likely currency reaction?

Answer: B. What moves currencies is the surprise relative to what was priced in — a smaller-than-expected cut is a hawkish surprise, even though the headline is technically a rate cut.

5. Which inflation regime is generally considered the most difficult for a central bank to manage?

Answer: B. Stagflation is hardest because fighting inflation with higher rates risks further weakening already-fragile growth — there's no easy lever to pull.

6. Why might a central bank's forward guidance move markets faster if the institution has high credibility?

Answer: B. A track record of following through on stated commitments means the market trusts (and prices in) the guidance more readily and quickly.

7. What happens to a currency's outlook when monetary policy is tightening while fiscal policy is simultaneously expansionary?

Answer: A. When the two forces point in opposite directions, the net currency impact becomes genuinely harder to call and depends on market judgment about which force dominates.
