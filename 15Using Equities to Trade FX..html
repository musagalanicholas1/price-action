Using Equities to Trade FX

Learn how global equity markets move currency prices, which stock indices matter most, and how the relationship between stocks and currencies plays out in the real world — including a concrete framework for using stock market signals in your day-to-day trading.

01 Foundations

Why Stock Markets Matter to Currency Traders

How international capital flows connect stock performance to exchange rates.

When foreign investors buy a country's stocks, they typically must first convert their own currency into the local currency to make the purchase — creating direct currency demand tied to equity inflows. Sustained foreign buying of a country's stock market is, in this sense, a sustained source of currency demand, separate from anything covered in the macro fundamentals modules.

03 Nuance

The Stock Market-Currency Relationship: Theory vs Reality

Which market leads, which follows, and how the relationship can flip over time.

The textbook theory (lesson 1) suggests rising local stocks should support the local currency through inflow demand. Reality is messier: a heavily export-weighted index (like the FTSE 100) can actually rise when the local currency weakens, since a weaker currency makes large exporters' overseas earnings worth more in local-currency terms once converted back.

The key lesson: check the composition of the specific index before assuming a simple "stocks up, currency up" relationship — export-heavy indices can behave in the opposite direction from what the basic theory predicts.

Global Co-Movement

Major equity markets worldwide also tend to move together to a significant degree, since global risk sentiment (the risk-on/risk-off theme from Modules 12 and 14) drives capital flows across borders simultaneously rather than country by country in isolation.

04 Case Study

Why the Nikkei and USD/JPY Move Together

The yen carry trade drives this relationship — and the 2024 unwind revealed its risks.

The Nikkei and USD/JPY have historically shown a notably positive correlation: when USD/JPY rises (yen weakens), the Nikkei often rises too, and vice versa. Two forces drive this:

  • Export sensitivity — many large Nikkei constituents are major exporters, similar to the FTSE dynamic in lesson 3, benefiting when the yen weakens.
  • The yen carry trade (Module 10) — yen-funded carry trades often finance positions in global equities, including Japanese stocks themselves, linking yen weakness/strength directly to equity flows.

The August 2024 carry trade unwind (Module 10, lesson 6) showed this relationship's downside vividly: a sharp yen strengthening move, triggered by a surprise BoJ hike, coincided with a severe Nikkei selloff as carry-funded positions were forced to deleverage — the same link that supports the relationship in calm times amplified the damage during the shock.

Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Using Equities to Trade FX

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. Why does foreign buying of a country's stock market create currency demand?

Answer: A. The currency conversion required to purchase local equities directly creates demand for that currency.

2. Why might a heavily export-weighted index like the FTSE 100 actually rise when GBP weakens?

Answer: A. Export-heavy companies often benefit from currency weakness through improved overseas earnings translation, which can override the simple "stocks up, currency up" theory.

3. What are the two main drivers behind the historically positive correlation between the Nikkei and USD/JPY?

Answer: A. Export sensitivity and the yen carry trade are the two structural forces behind this historically observed relationship.

4. What did the August 2024 carry trade unwind reveal about the Nikkei/USD-JPY relationship?

Answer: A. The carry-trade mechanism that links the two in normal conditions worked in reverse during the unwind, amplifying losses across both the yen and Japanese equities simultaneously.
