Currency Crosses

Most forex traders limit themselves to the seven major dollar pairs — meaning every trade they take is really just one bet: is the dollar strong or weak today? Currency crosses free you from that constraint, letting you trade the relationship between any two economies directly.

01 Foundations

What Are Currency Crosses?

Why the FX market is obsessed with the U.S. dollar, and why trading crosses gives you more opportunities than sticking to the majors.

A currency cross (or cross pair) is any currency pair that doesn't include the U.S. dollar — EUR/GBP, AUD/JPY, GBP/CAD, and so on. The "majors" (EUR/USD, USD/JPY, GBP/USD, USD/CHF, USD/CAD, AUD/USD, NZD/USD) all share one currency in common: USD. That shared structure is exactly the limitation crosses solve.

Why the Dollar Dominates

The U.S. dollar sits on one side of the overwhelming majority of global FX transactions, a legacy of its role as the world's primary reserve and invoicing currency (revisited in depth in Module 10). This is convenient for liquidity, but it also means a trader who only trades majors is, in every single trade, really making one underlying bet over and over: dollar strength or dollar weakness.

Why this matters: trading only majors means your opportunity set rises and falls entirely with how active the dollar is. Crosses open up dozens of additional relationships that can be trending and tradeable even during a quiet, directionless period for the dollar itself.
02 Hidden Mechanics

The Hidden Force Moving Your Dollar Pairs

Cross pair movements can affect your dollar trades even when you never look at a cross.

Because every major pair shares the dollar, and crosses are mathematically derived from combinations of majors, a sharp move in a cross pair can quietly pull on one of your dollar trades — even if you never glance at the cross itself.

For example, EUR/GBP is mathematically related to EUR/USD and GBP/USD: EUR/GBP ≈ EUR/USD ÷ GBP/USD. A sharp, GBP-specific move (a surprise BoE decision, for instance) shows up in EUR/GBP and GBP/USD simultaneously — and through that relationship, can put pressure on EUR/USD even though nothing fundamentally changed about the euro or the dollar that day.

Practical implication: if you trade dollar majors exclusively, keeping half an eye on the relevant cross pairs can explain otherwise puzzling moves in your own trades.
03 Decision Support

Which Trade Do You Take? Ask the Cross.

Use a cross pair to identify which of two similar dollar pair signals is actually the stronger trade.

Suppose your analysis flags both a long EUR/USD setup and a long GBP/USD setup at the same time. Both can't be your "best" trade — and taking both means you're really just taking one large dollar-weakness bet split across two tickets. The relevant cross, EUR/GBP, tells you which currency is actually outperforming the other.

This removes a common beginner trap: mistaking "the dollar is weak today" for "I found two great independent setups." The cross pair reveals whether you actually have two distinct opportunities or one dollar trade wearing two different tickers.

04 Application

How to Trade Your Fundamental View With a Cross Pair

Match the strongest currency against the weakest and trade that relationship directly — no USD view required.

Module 08 covered building a fundamental thesis. Often, that thesis isn't really about the dollar at all — it might be a view that the Australian economy is outperforming Japan's, with the RBA on a different policy path than the BoJ. A cross pair like AUD/JPY lets you express that view directly, without taking on any unrelated USD exposure or noise.

A Simple Process

  1. Rank currencies by your fundamental conviction — strongest expected performer at one end, weakest at the other (a full ranking framework using fundamentals appears again in Module 17).
  2. Pair the strongest against the weakest for the cleanest expression of the view, assuming a liquid cross exists between them.
  3. Apply price action and multi-timeframe analysis (Modules 01–04) to time the actual entry on that cross.
Why this can be cleaner than trading majors: pairing your strongest-conviction currency against your weakest-conviction one isolates the relationship you actually have an opinion about, rather than diluting it with a dollar view you may not hold at all.
05 Practical Filtering

Which Crosses Are Actually Worth Trading?

Which pairs to start with, which to approach with caution, and which to avoid until you know what you're doing.

Start here — most liquidEUR/GBP, EUR/JPY, GBP/JPY, AUD/JPYTight spreads, reliable liquidity, well-established trending behavior

A simple starting rule: if you wouldn't recognize the spread on a cross as reasonable compared to a major pair, treat that as a sign to either trade smaller or skip it until you've built more experience.
Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Currency Crosses

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. What defines a currency cross?

Answer: B. A cross pair is any pair that excludes USD — EUR/GBP, AUD/JPY, GBP/CAD, and similar combinations.

2. Why can a trader who only trades dollar majors really be making one repeated underlying bet?

Answer: A. Since USD sits on one side of every major pair, trading only majors means repeatedly betting on the same underlying dollar direction.

3. Both EUR/USD and GBP/USD look bullish at the same time. What can checking EUR/GBP reveal?

Answer: B. The cross isolates which of the two non-dollar currencies is genuinely the stronger performer, revealing whether both signals are really just one dollar trade in disguise.

4. Why might a trader use a cross pair like AUD/JPY to express a fundamental view, rather than a dollar pair?

Answer: A. A cross lets a trader express a relative view between two specific economies directly, without taking on unrelated USD exposure.

5. What's a practical, beginner-friendly way to judge whether a cross is worth trading?

Answer: B. Spread width is a quick, practical proxy for liquidity and trading comfort — wide, unreliable spreads are a reasonable signal to proceed cautiously or avoid the pair for now.
