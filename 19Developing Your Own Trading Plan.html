Module 19 of the Trading Curriculum

Developing Your Own Trading Plan

Most traders jump straight to strategies and wonder why nothing sticks. This module gives you the foundation they skipped: a complete, personalized trading plan built around who you actually are, how much time you have, and what you can realistically risk.

01 Foundations

What Is a Trading Plan and Why Does It Matter?

The only thing that separates trading from gambling.

A trading plan is a written document that specifies how you will make trading decisions before you make them — covering which markets you trade, what setups you take, how much you risk, and how you behave under stress. It is distinct from a single strategy: a strategy is one tool inside a plan, while the plan governs everything around that tool, including risk, mindset, and review process.

The core distinction: gambling accepts risk without a structured edge or process for managing it. Trading, properly defined, is the deliberate, planned acceptance of risk in pursuit of a structured, repeatable edge — and a written plan is what makes that distinction real rather than aspirational.
02 Self-Assessment

Know Yourself: Building Your Trader Profile

Answer questions about yourself before writing a single trading rule.

Time

How much screen time do you actually have?

Not aspirationally — realistically, given work, family, and other commitments (directly informs the timeframe choice from Module 04).

Capital

What capital can you risk without affecting your life?

Only true risk capital belongs in a trading account — covered in depth in Module 20.

Temperament

How do you handle uncertainty and loss?

Some traders tolerate frequent small losses calmly; others find that psychologically corrosive even when the math is sound.

Goals

What are you actually trying to achieve?

Supplemental income, full-time replacement, or skill-building — each implies a different realistic pace and risk tolerance.

Decision speed

Are you a fast or deliberate decision-maker?

This maps directly onto the trading-style choice from Module 21 (scalping vs. position trading).

Markets

Which markets and sessions actually fit your life?

Time zone and session overlap (Module 04) matter as much as market choice itself.

The output of this lesson should be a short, written Trader Profile — a paragraph or two honestly describing your time, capital, temperament, and goals — that becomes the foundation the rest of your plan is built on.

What belongs in it, and what beginners typically get wrong.

04 Discipline

Sticking to Your Trading Plan

The hard part — building discipline that gives your strategy a real chance to work.

  • Pre-commit before the trade, not during it. Decisions made calmly before entry are far more reliable than ones made under the emotional pressure of an open position.
  • Use a pre-trade checklist. A short, written checklist run through before every entry reduces the chance of impulsively skipping a rule.
  • Separate "the plan was wrong" from "I didn't follow the plan." Most early-stage losses come from the second category, not the first — and they require completely different fixes.
  • Track deviations explicitly in your trading journal (Module 22) so patterns of rule-breaking become visible rather than invisible.
A common trap: after a string of losses on a plan that's actually sound, the temptation is to abandon it entirely rather than examine whether the losses came from a flawed plan or from not following it. Module 21 (Build Your Own Trading System) returns to this distinction with a structured framework.
05 Evolution

Your Trading Plan Is a Living Document

What to expect in your first 90 days, and habits that support long-term growth.

A trading plan should evolve as you accumulate real evidence about what works for you — but that evolution needs to be deliberate and evidence-based, not a reaction to the most recent win or loss.

PeriodWhat to focus on
First 30 daysExecuting the plan exactly as written, gathering data — resist the urge to tweak rules this early
Days 30–60Reviewing journal data (Module 22) for patterns in execution and rule-following
Days 60–90Making the first deliberate, evidence-based adjustment to one specific component of the plan
One change at a time: revising multiple components of your plan simultaneously makes it impossible to know which change actually caused any subsequent improvement or decline — a principle revisited in Module 22's journal review framework.
Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Developing Your Own Trading Plan

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. What's the key distinction between a trading plan and a trading strategy?

Answer: B. A plan is the broader framework — including risk rules and review process — within which a specific strategy operates.

2. Why does building a Trader Profile come before writing specific trading rules?

Answer: A. Self-assessment ensures the resulting plan is realistically suited to the trader's actual constraints and personality.

3. Which of these is one of the five core components of a trading plan?

Answer: A. Risk rules — including per-trade risk and position sizing — are one of the five essential components, alongside market/timeframe selection, setup criteria, trade management, and review process.

4. Why is it important to distinguish "the plan was wrong" from "I didn't follow the plan"?

Answer: A. Conflating execution failures with strategy failures leads to abandoning sound plans or persisting with flawed ones — recognizing which is which is essential.

5. Why does the 90-day framework recommend changing only one component of a plan at a time?

Answer: A. Isolating one variable at a time preserves the ability to attribute results to a specific change, supporting genuine evidence-based refinement.
