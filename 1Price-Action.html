Module 01 of the Trading Curriculum

Price Action Trading

Price action is how experienced traders cut through the noise and read what a chart is actually saying. Build an analytical framework from the ground up — market structure, supply and demand, entry setups, confluence, and multi-timeframe analysis — that works on forex, stocks, gold, silver, crypto, and everything in between.

01 Foundations

What Is Price Action Trading?

Why stripping your chart down to bare price might be the most powerful upgrade you make as a trader.

Price action trading is the practice of making trading decisions based on the actual movement of price over time — the highs, lows, opens, closes, and the shapes they form on a chart — rather than relying on lagging indicators built on top of that price. Every indicator you've ever seen (moving averages, MACD, Stochastic, RSI) is derived from price. Price action trading skips the derivation and reads the source data directly.

That might sound like a stylistic preference. It isn't. It changes what kind of information you have access to. An indicator smooths, lags, and summarizes. It tells you what price did a few candles ago, compressed into a single number. Price action tells you what's happening right now, in granular detail: who's in control, where they're losing conviction, and where the next decision point is likely to occur.

Why It Works Across Every Market

Price action is asset-agnostic because it isn't reading a company's earnings, a country's interest rate, or a coin's tokenomics — it's reading behavior. Every liquid, freely-traded market is a continuous auction between buyers and sellers. That auction produces the same structural patterns whether the ticker says EUR/USD, XAU/USD, AAPL, or BTC/USD: swings, ranges, breakouts, retests, and reversals. Learn to read that behavior once, and you can apply it to any chart that has enough participants fighting over price.

What Price Action Trading Is Not

  • It is not "no indicators, ever." Many price action traders use a handful of tools (ATR for volatility, volume for confirmation) — the rule is that price comes first, and tools support the read rather than replace it.
  • It is not pattern memorization. Recognizing a "pin bar" or "engulfing candle" in isolation is trivia. Price action trading is about reading what a pattern means in its location — a skill you'll build starting in lesson five.
  • It is not a guarantee. Price action gives you probability and context, not certainty. It tells you where the more likely outcome lies, not where the only outcome lies.
The core shift: Stop asking "what is this indicator telling me?" and start asking "what is price telling me, right here, right now?" Every lesson in this module trains that second question.
02 Foundations

Market Structure: Trends and Ranges

The sequence of highs and lows tells you who's winning between buyers and sellers — before you ever look for a single trade.

Market structure is the skeleton underneath every chart. It is simply the sequence of swing highs and swing lows price has left behind, read in order. That sequence falls into one of three states, and every single thing you do next in this course depends on identifying which one you're looking at.

The Three Structural States

StateWhat's happeningSequence pattern
UptrendBuyers consistently push price to new highs and defend each pullback at a higher level than the last.Higher Highs (HH) + Higher Lows (HL)
DowntrendSellers consistently push price to new lows and cap each bounce at a lower level than the last.Lower Highs (LH) + Lower Lows (LL)
RangeNeither side can take control. Price oscillates between a ceiling and a floor.Roughly Equal Highs + Equal Lows

Why Structure Comes Before Everything Else

Market structure is a filter, not a trade signal by itself. Before you even consider an entry, structure answers one question: is this a market where I should be looking for trend-continuation setups, or one where I should be looking for range-boundary setups? Taking a "buy the pullback" trend setup inside a range, or a "fade the edge" range setup inside a strong trend, is one of the most common ways beginners lose money — not because their entry was wrong, but because they applied the right tool to the wrong environment.

The Break of Structure (BOS) and Change of Character (CHoCH)

Two terms you'll see constantly once you start studying structure on real charts:

  • Break of Structure (BOS): price continues in the direction of the existing trend by breaking the most recent swing point in that same direction — confirmation the trend is still intact.
  • Change of Character (CHoCH): price breaks a swing point against the prevailing trend for the first time — the earliest warning that control may be shifting from one side to the other.

A CHoCH doesn't guarantee a reversal. It guarantees that something about the trend's rhythm has changed, and that you should pay closer attention before assuming "more of the same."

Practical takeaway: Before opening any chart to look for a trade, your first task is always the same: label the last 5–8 swing points, decide HH/HL, LH/LL, or range — then choose your strategy to match.
03 Foundations

Identifying Swing Highs and Swing Lows

The one skill every other price action concept depends on.

A swing high is a candle (or short cluster of candles) whose high is higher than the highs of the candles immediately before and after it. A swing low is the mirror image: a low that is lower than the candles immediately surrounding it. That's the textbook definition — but the part that actually matters for trading is deciding which swing points are significant enough to act on, because on any real chart, the market produces far more minor wiggles than meaningful turning points.

Grading Significance: Three Practical Filters

  1. Magnitude of the move into and out of the point. A swing preceded and followed by a large, decisive move carries more weight than one formed by a few small candles.
  2. Time spent forming the point. A swing high that took several candles to roll over (multiple tests, hesitation) usually means more than a single-candle spike.
  3. Timeframe of confirmation. A swing visible on the 4-hour chart matters more to overall structure than one only visible on the 5-minute chart. Always grade significance relative to the timeframe you're trading.

The Fractal Rule

A simple, mechanical method many traders use: a swing high is confirmed when there are at least two lower highs on each side of it; a swing low is confirmed when there are at least two higher lows on each side of it. This is sometimes called a "fractal" or "pivot" definition, and it's the basis for most automated swing-detection indicators. The tradeoff: confirmation only arrives a couple of candles after the swing point has formed, since you need the candles on the right side to exist first.

The repainting trap: Because confirmation is always a little late, swing points you mark in real time can shift as new candles print. Don't anchor a trading decision to a swing point that hasn't had at least one or two candles confirm it on the relevant timeframe.

Why This Skill Is the Foundation

Market structure (lesson 2) is built entirely from swing points. Supply and demand zones (lesson 4) originate at swing points. Trendlines connect swing points. Stop-loss placement (a later module) is almost always anchored just beyond a swing point. If you can't reliably and consistently identify which highs and lows matter, every downstream skill in this course inherits that error.

04 Structure & Levels

Beyond Support and Resistance: Supply and Demand Zones

Why supply and demand zones are different from ordinary support and resistance — and how to grade their quality.

Traditional support and resistance treats a level as a single horizontal line: a price that has been "tested" before. Supply and demand trading reframes the idea. Instead of a line, you mark a zone — the actual range of candles where a sharp, decisive move began — because that's the area where a meaningful imbalance between buyers and sellers actually occurred. Price rarely reverses at the exact same tick twice; it reverses somewhere inside the zone where that imbalance lives.

What Creates a Zone

A demand zone forms where aggressive buying overwhelmed available selling and price launched upward, leaving the origin candles behind. A supply zone is the mirror: aggressive selling overwhelmed buying, and price dropped away from the origin candles. The stronger and faster the departure, the more unfilled orders are presumed to remain resting in that zone — which is the working theory for why price tends to react there again.

Grading Zone Quality

FactorHigher qualityLower quality
Departure speedSharp, fast move away (often with a strong, large-bodied candle)Slow, grinding move away
Time at originFew candles forming the zone — price didn't lingerMany candles consolidating before departure
Number of prior testsFresh, untested zoneAlready tested multiple times (orders likely depleted)
Approach into the zonePrice approaches in a clean, momentum-driven movePrice grinds in sideways
The freshness principle: A zone's reactive power is a depleting resource. Every time price returns to a zone and reacts, some of the resting orders that caused the reaction get filled. A zone tested for the fourth time is structurally weaker than the same zone tested for the first time, all else equal.

Marking a Zone Step by Step

  1. Find a sharp, impulsive move on your chart.
  2. Trace back to the candle(s) immediately preceding that move — the "base."
  3. Draw your zone from the extreme wick of the base to the body of the last consolidating candle (not the full wick-to-wick range, which is usually too wide to be useful).
  4. Label it supply or demand based on the direction price departed.
  5. Note how many times it's been retested since — downgrade quality with each test.
05 Reading Price

Reading Candles in Context

The same candlestick pattern can mean everything in one location and nothing in another.

This is the lesson where most beginners' approach to price action gets corrected. Candlestick pattern lists (hammer, shooting star, engulfing, doji) are everywhere online, almost always presented as if the pattern alone is the signal. It isn't. A candlestick pattern is a snapshot of who won a single battle between buyers and sellers. Its meaning depends entirely on where that battle took place.

The Three Questions Every Candle Must Answer

  1. Where did it form? At a supply/demand zone, a prior swing point, a trendline, a round number — or in open space with no nearby level?
  2. What happened just before it? Did price arrive via a strong impulsive move (more meaningful) or a slow grind (less meaningful)?
  3. What is the higher-timeframe context? Is this candle aligned with the dominant trend, or fighting against it?

A Working Vocabulary, Used Correctly

PatternWhat it showsWhere it matters most
Pin bar / rejection wickPrice probed beyond a level and was firmly rejected backAt the edge of a zone, not mid-range
Engulfing candleOne side fully overwhelmed the entire prior candle's rangeAfter a pullback into a zone, in the direction of the larger trend
Inside barContraction — the market is compressing before its next moveMid-trend continuation pause, or right before a breakout
Doji / indecision candleBuyers and sellers fought to a standstillMeaningful at a key level after a strong move; meaningless in a slow chop
Common mistake: Trading every pin bar you see on a chart. A pattern in open space, away from any meaningful level, is statistical noise — not a setup.
06 Setups

Price Action Setups: The Big Three

Three entry setups that show up on every market and every timeframe.

Nearly every price-action trade you'll ever take is a variation on one of three underlying setups. Learn these well and you have a complete entry toolkit.

Setup 1

Zone Reversal

Price returns to a fresh, high-quality supply or demand zone and shows a rejection candle (pin bar, engulfing) in the direction back into the range.

Setup 2

Break-and-Retest

Price breaks a key structural level, then returns to retest that level as new support/resistance before continuing in the breakout direction.

Setup 3

Trend Continuation Pullback

In an established trend, price pulls back to a prior swing point or moving zone of value, then resumes in the direction of the dominant trend.

Entry, Stop, and Target — The Universal Template

SetupEntry triggerStop placementTarget
Zone ReversalClose of rejection candle, or break of its high/lowJust beyond the far edge of the zoneNext opposing structural level
Break-and-RetestHold of the retested level with a confirming candleJust on the far side of the retested levelMeasured move or next structural level
Trend PullbackResumption candle off the pullback low/highJust beyond the pullback's extremePrior swing high/low in trend direction

Notice the pattern: in every setup, your stop goes where the trade idea is objectively proven wrong, and your target is the next visible structural level — not an arbitrary number of pips. This logic recurs throughout the entire curriculum.

07 Filtering Quality

Confluence: Stacking the Odds in Your Favor

Five factors pointing at the same level is a completely different trade from one.

Confluence is the presence of multiple, independent reasons to expect price to react at the same level. No single factor in price action is reliable enough to trade on its own with consistency. What separates a trade worth taking from one worth skipping is usually the number of independent factors stacking at the same price.

Common Confluence Factors

  • A supply or demand zone
  • A prior swing high or swing low
  • A round, psychologically significant number (1.1000, 2000.00, etc.)
  • A Fibonacci retracement level (38.2%, 50%, 61.8%)
  • A trendline or channel boundary
  • A moving average sitting at the same price
  • Alignment with the higher-timeframe trend direction

Building a Simple Confluence Score

Before entering, count how many independent factors line up at your level. A practical scale:

ScoreInterpretation
1 factorLow conviction — consider skipping or using a reduced size
2–3 factorsStandard setup — tradeable with normal risk
4+ factorsHigh-conviction setup — your best opportunities tend to cluster here
Independence matters more than quantity. Five factors that are really just restating the same swing high in different language aren't five factors — they're one. Genuine confluence comes from tools that derive their signal differently (structure, Fibonacci, psychology, trend) lining up by coincidence at the same price.
08 Context

Price Action Across Multiple Timeframes

Reading the same asset across three timeframes to identify where, when, and which direction to trade.

A single timeframe shows you one slice of the auction. The same price can look like a clean uptrend on the 4-hour chart and a chaotic, directionless mess on the 5-minute chart at the exact same moment — both readings are "correct," but only one is complete. Multi-timeframe price action analysis fixes this by assigning each timeframe a specific job.

Assigning the Roles

TierTypical pairing examplesQuestion it answers
Higher (bias)Weekly/Daily, Daily/4H, 4H/1HWhat is the dominant trend, and which direction should I even be looking?
Middle (setup)Daily, 4H, 1HWhere is the nearest high-quality zone or level in that direction?
Lower (trigger)1H, 15M, 5MWhat candle or micro-structure confirms the entry right now?

The Discipline This Demands

The hardest part of multi-timeframe analysis isn't the technique — it's resisting the urge to take a lower-timeframe setup that contradicts the higher-timeframe bias just because it "looks good." A textbook reversal pattern on the 15-minute chart, against a strong 4-hour downtrend, is a lower-probability trade than the same pattern aligned with that trend. Always let the higher timeframe have the final veto.

A note on conflict: When timeframes genuinely disagree (HTF up, MTF ranging, LTF down), the correct response is usually patience — wait for clarity rather than forcing a trade through the noise.
09 Synthesis

Summary: Price Action Trading

Pulling every concept into one coherent framework.

You now have a complete price action framework, built in the order it should actually be applied:

  1. Read the structure first. Trend up, trend down, or range — this decides what kind of setup you're even allowed to look for.
  2. Find the swing points. They define structure, and they're the anchor for everything that follows.
  3. Map supply and demand zones. These are your areas of interest — where price is statistically more likely to react.
  4. Read candles only in context of where they form, not what they look like in isolation.
  5. Match the setup to the environment — Zone Reversal, Break-and-Retest, or Trend Pullback.
  6. Score confluence before pulling the trigger — more independent factors, higher conviction.
  7. Confirm across timeframes — bias from the top, location from the middle, timing from the bottom.

What's Next

Market Environment (Module 3) refines how you tell a retracement from a reversal. Multiple Timeframe Analysis (Module 4) expands lesson 8 into a full workspace and routine. Treat this module as the operating system the rest of the curriculum runs on.

What to Practice

  • Pull up 10 charts across different assets. For each, label structure, mark the 3 most significant swing points, and identify one supply/demand zone — before you do anything else.
  • Find five historical examples of each Big Three setup on charts you didn't trade live, and write down what confluence factors were present.
  • Practice the three-tier timeframe stack on a single pair for a week, writing down the bias, the zone, and the trigger separately each day.
Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Price Action Trading

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. A market prints a Lower Low for the first time inside an otherwise clean uptrend of Higher Highs and Higher Lows. What does this represent?

Answer: B. Breaking structure against the prevailing trend for the first time is a Change of Character — it signals the rhythm may be shifting, but it does not guarantee a full reversal on its own.

2. Why is a supply or demand zone drawn as a range of candles rather than a single horizontal line?

Answer: A. The zone represents the actual area where the buy/sell imbalance occurred, giving price room to react anywhere inside it rather than at one exact price.

3. A textbook bullish pin bar forms in the dead middle of a range, with no nearby zone, swing point, or trendline. What should you do?

Answer: B. Candlestick patterns derive their meaning from where they occur. Without a meaningful level behind it, the pattern alone offers no real edge.

4. Which of the Big Three setups relies on a previously broken level flipping roles (e.g., old resistance becoming new support)?

Answer: B. The Break-and-Retest setup specifically depends on a broken structural level being retested and holding in its new role before continuation.

5. Five different tools — a Fibonacci level, a moving average, a round number, a swing low, and a trendline — all sit within a few pips of each other. What does this represent?

Answer: A. Genuine confluence is several independently-derived tools agreeing on the same price — this is exactly the kind of setup that tends to attract higher-conviction trades.

6. In a three-tier multi-timeframe stack (Higher / Middle / Lower), what is the job of the lower timeframe?

Answer: C. The higher timeframe sets bias, the middle timeframe locates the setup zone, and the lower timeframe is used only for precise entry timing.
