The U.S. Dollar Index

Measure the U.S. dollar's strength and understand why it matters across almost every market, from currencies and commodities to stocks and bonds. Learn how to read dollar signals, compare different measures of dollar strength, and apply the Dollar Smile Theory to market behavior.

01 Foundations

The Global Role of the U.S. Dollar

The key aspects of the dollar's global role, and what gives it such enduring appeal.

The U.S. dollar functions as the world's primary reserve currency, the dominant currency for invoicing international trade (even between two non-U.S. countries), and the most widely held currency in global central bank reserves. This outsized role is why, as Module 09 explained, the dollar sits on one side of the large majority of all FX transactions globally.

02 The Index

What is the US Dollar Index (USDX)?

Which currencies are in the basket, and how forex traders use it.

The U.S. Dollar Index (commonly ticker DXY) measures the dollar's value against a fixed basket of six major currencies, weighted by their historical significance in U.S. trade.

CurrencyApproximate weight
EUR~57.6%
JPY~13.6%
GBP~11.9%
CAD~9.1%
SEK~4.2%
CHF~3.6%
A key limitation: the heavy EUR weighting means the DXY behaves, in practice, substantially like an inverse EUR/USD chart — it's a useful but imperfect proxy for "the dollar" against the entire world, since several major U.S. trading partners (China, Mexico) aren't represented at all.
03 Mechanics

How to Read the US Dollar Index

What its base value of 100 means, and how to interpret readings above and below it.

The index was set to a base value of 100 in March 1973. A reading above 100 means the dollar has strengthened against the basket since that base period; a reading below 100 means it has weakened. The index itself moves as a weighted geometric average of the basket currencies' moves against the dollar.

04 Application

How to Use the USDX for Forex Trading

How it correlates with EUR/USD and other major pairs.

Because of the index's heavy euro weighting, DXY and EUR/USD typically move in a near-mirror, inversely correlated pattern. Traders often use DXY as a quick proxy check: if DXY is breaking a key level while EUR/USD is approaching the corresponding level on its own chart, that's a form of confluence (Module 01, lesson 7) worth noting.

Practical useHow it helps
Quick broad-dollar checkA single glance at DXY direction substitutes for checking all seven majors individually
Divergence spottingIf DXY is strong but a specific dollar pair lags, that pair's own currency may be unusually strong/weak in its own right
Confluence with EUR/USDDXY levels often align closely with corresponding EUR/USD levels given the heavy weighting
05 Alternative Measures

Trade Weighted Dollar Index

How the Federal Reserve built it, and why it differs from the USDX.

The Federal Reserve publishes its own broad Trade Weighted Dollar Index, built from a wider and periodically updated basket of currencies weighted by actual current U.S. trade flows — including major partners like China and Mexico that the DXY excludes entirely.

Why this matters: the DXY and the Fed's Trade Weighted Index can tell different stories about "dollar strength" at the same time, since one reflects a fixed, decades-old basket and the other reflects current, broader trade relationships.
06 Alternative Measures

Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index

How it differs from the DXY, and how to access it for free.

The Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index (ticker BBDXY) tracks the dollar against a basket of ten currencies, periodically rebalanced based on liquidity and trade significance — a broader and more frequently updated alternative to the DXY's fixed six-currency basket.

07 Framework

The Dollar Smile Theory

Why the dollar strengthens both during crises and economic booms, and weakens in between.

The Dollar Smile Theory, developed by strategist Stephen Jen, explains a pattern that confuses many beginner traders: the dollar can strengthen in two opposite-seeming environments — a global crisis (safe-haven demand, Module 07) and a strong U.S. economic boom (capital attracted by growth and yield) — while tending to weaken in the calmer middle ground between the two extremes.

Practical use: before assuming "the dollar should weaken because the U.S. economy looks shaky," check which side of the smile you're actually on — a global crisis scenario can still produce dollar strength via safe-haven flows even alongside weakening U.S. data.
Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: The U.S. Dollar Index

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. Why does the DXY behave, in practice, much like an inverse EUR/USD chart?

Answer: A. With the euro weighted at roughly 57.6% of the basket, the DXY's moves are heavily dominated by EUR/USD's inverse behavior.

2. What does a DXY reading above 100 indicate?

Answer: B. The DXY's base value of 100 was set in March 1973 — readings above that level indicate dollar strength relative to that base.

3. Why might the Fed's Trade Weighted Dollar Index tell a different story than the DXY at the same time?

Answer: A. The Trade Weighted Index reflects a broader, current set of trade relationships, while the DXY uses a fixed, narrower six-currency basket.

4. According to the Dollar Smile Theory, in which two environments does the dollar tend to strengthen?

Answer: B. The Dollar Smile Theory describes dollar strength at both extremes — crisis-driven safe-haven flows and boom-driven capital inflows — with relative weakness in the calmer middle ground.

5. What is one practical use of watching the DXY alongside individual dollar pairs?

Answer: B. The DXY is useful as a fast overall-dollar gauge and for spotting when an individual pair is behaving unusually relative to the broader dollar trend.
