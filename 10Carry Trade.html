Module 10 of the Trading Curriculum

Carry Trade

The carry trade is one of the most widely used strategies in FX. This module breaks it down from the ground up: how it works, how to find viable setups, what makes it succeed or fail, and how to manage the risks that come with holding a position for weeks or months.

01 Foundations

What Is the Carry Trade?

One of the most popular strategies used by forex traders and big-money investors around the world.

A carry trade involves borrowing (or holding short exposure) in a currency with a low interest rate, and using those funds to hold a long position in a currency with a higher interest rate — earning the difference between the two rates, known as the interest rate differential, as long as the position is held.

This isn't unique to forex — the same logic applies to bonds, emerging market debt, and other rate-sensitive assets globally. In FX specifically, the carry trade has been a persistent, widely-used strategy precisely because interest rate differentials between major economies can remain wide and stable for extended periods.

02 Mechanics

How Does the Carry Trade Work in Forex?

Daily swap credits and debits, leverage, and what positive vs. negative carry looks like in practice.

In the retail spot forex market, you don't literally borrow and deposit cash overnight — your broker handles this through a daily swap (or rollover) charge or credit, applied to any position held overnight, reflecting the interest rate differential between the two currencies in the pair.

PositionResult
Long the higher-yielding currency, short the lower-yielding onePositive carry — you receive a small swap credit each night held
Long the lower-yielding currency, short the higher-yielding oneNegative carry — you pay a small swap debit each night held

The Role of Leverage

Because daily interest differentials are typically small in percentage terms, carry traders historically used significant leverage to make the accumulated swap meaningful relative to account size. Leverage amplifies both the swap income and the price risk — a critical point picked up again in lesson 7.

03 Setup Identification

Finding a Carry Trade: Criteria and Currency Pairs

The two criteria every carry trade must meet, and which currencies have historically served as funding currencies.

The Two Criteria

  1. A meaningful, stable interest rate differential between the two currencies — wide enough to matter, and from central banks not expected to converge sharply in the near term.
  2. A stable or appreciating target currency — since price moves can easily overwhelm the swap income earned (this is explored fully in lesson 5).

Historical Funding Currencies

CurrencyWhy it's historically used as a funding currency
JPYDecades of ultra-low or negative Bank of Japan rates
CHFPersistently low Swiss National Bank rates and a stable, low-inflation economy
EURAn extended period of low/negative ECB rates in the 2010s and early 2020s
This shifts over time. Funding-currency status is a function of the current rate cycle, not a permanent label — a theme returned to in the final lesson of this module.
04 Case Study

A Carry Trade in Action: The USD/JPY Case Study

How the USD/JPY carry trade played out from 2022 to 2023.

Starting in 2022, the Federal Reserve embarked on one of its most aggressive rate-hiking cycles in decades to combat surging inflation, while the Bank of Japan held its policy rate near zero, maintaining its long-standing ultra-accommodative stance. The result was one of the widest USD-JPY interest rate differentials seen in decades.

USD/JPY trended persistently higher through this period, rewarding carry traders both through swap income and substantial currency appreciation — an unusually favorable combination, since (as lesson 5 explains) carry trades don't always get both at once.

05 Conditions

When Carry Trades Work and When They Don't

How to think about the market conditions behind the difference.

ConditionCarry trades tend to...
Low volatility, "risk-on" environmentWork well — stable conditions let the carry accumulate without being overwhelmed by price swings
Wide, stable, or widening rate differentialWork well — more income to earn, less risk of the trade's basic premise reversing suddenly
Rising volatility / risk-off shockStruggle or reverse sharply — see the unwind dynamic in the next lesson
Narrowing rate differentialLose their basic rationale — less income to earn, less reason for capital to stay positioned
The core vulnerability: carry trades earn small, steady income but are exposed to potentially large, fast currency moves. A single bad week of price action can erase months of accumulated swap income — the strategy's reward and risk profile are fundamentally asymmetric.
06 Tail Risk

Carry Trade Unwind: When It All Goes Wrong

The August 2024 yen carry trade unwind shook global markets in a matter of days.

A carry trade "unwind" occurs when a large number of traders holding the same crowded carry position close out simultaneously — typically triggered by a shock that makes the trade suddenly look far riskier, such as an unexpected funding-currency rate hike or a sharp spike in global risk aversion.

Why the Damage Spreads Beyond FX

Because carry trades are often funded with leverage and used to finance positions in other asset classes (including equities), a forced unwind can trigger margin calls and selling well beyond the currency market itself — which is exactly what happened in August 2024, when yen-funded equity positions globally were forced to deleverage alongside the FX move.

The lesson: a crowded carry trade carries a hidden tail risk that's invisible during calm periods and can resolve violently and suddenly once a trigger appears.
07 Risk Management

Carry Trade Risk Management

Sizing, stop-losses, voluntary exit signals, and protecting accumulated swap income.

  • Size conservatively. Given the leverage often used to make carry meaningful, position sizing principles from Module 14 apply with extra discipline here.
  • Use a real stop-loss. A carry trade is still a directional FX position and needs the same stop-loss discipline as any other trade (Module 16) — the swap income is a bonus, not a substitute for risk control.
  • Watch positioning data. Extreme, one-sided positioning in a popular carry pair (covered in Module 12's COT report lessons) is a warning sign of unwind risk building.
  • Watch volatility indices. A sharp rise in the VIX or similar risk gauges (Module 12) often precedes carry trade stress.
  • Consider partial profit-taking on accumulated swap income periodically, rather than letting it ride entirely on the position's continued survival.
08 Evolving Cycles

Nothing Lasts Forever (Especially a Good Carry Trade)

How funding and target currency roles shift over time, and how to stay current as central banks move through their cycles.

The funding currency in today's most popular carry trade is whichever currency currently has the lowest rate among liquid, widely-traded options — and that label changes as central banks move through their own hiking and cutting cycles. A currency that funded carry trades for a decade can become the target currency once its central bank begins hiking aggressively (and vice versa).

Staying current means regularly revisiting central bank policy paths (Module 05) rather than assuming the funding/target relationships that worked in the past will hold indefinitely. The carry trade itself never disappears as a strategy — but which specific pair expresses it best is always evolving.

Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Carry Trade

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. What is the basic structure of a carry trade?

Answer: B. A carry trade is structured to earn the interest rate differential by holding the higher-yielding currency funded against the lower-yielding one.

2. What does "positive carry" mean for a retail forex position?

Answer: B. Positive carry means receiving a small nightly credit, reflecting the rate differential in your favor.

3. In the 2022–2023 USD/JPY carry trade example, what created such a wide interest rate differential?

Answer: B. The Fed's aggressive hiking cycle against the BoJ's continued near-zero policy created one of the widest USD-JPY rate differentials in decades.

4. Why can a single bad week of price action erase months of accumulated carry trade income?

Answer: A. Carry trades earn small, steady income but remain fully exposed to currency price risk, which can move far faster than the income accumulates.

5. What triggered the August 2024 yen carry trade unwind, and why did its effects spread beyond FX?

Answer: A. The shock made the yen carry trade suddenly unprofitable, and because leveraged yen-funded positions extended into other assets (including equities), the unwind spread well beyond FX.

6. Why is the identity of "the" funding currency in carry trades not permanent?

Answer: A. A currency's funding or target role depends on where its central bank sits in the broader rate cycle, which changes over time as policy shifts.
