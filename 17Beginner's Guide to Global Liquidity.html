Module 17 of the Trading Curriculum

Beginner's Guide to Global Liquidity

Markets are driven by liquidity flows more than fundamentals in the short to medium term. You can track these flows using free, publicly available data. This module teaches you to observe what's happening with liquidity and position accordingly.

01 Foundations

Reading the Market's Plumbing: A Beginner's Guide to Global Liquidity Analysis

Five global liquidity indicators that reveal when to be aggressive or defensive.

"Liquidity" here refers to the overall availability of money and credit moving through the global financial system — separate from, but interacting closely with, the fundamentals frameworks from Modules 05–08. When liquidity is abundant, asset prices across the board tend to be supported; when liquidity tightens, even fundamentally sound assets can struggle. This module introduces five practical, freely available gauges of that underlying condition.

02 Funding Stress

Understanding Overnight Lending Stress: SOFR vs IORB

A real-time stress detector for dollar funding markets.

SOFR (Secured Overnight Financing Rate) reflects the cost of overnight borrowing secured by Treasury collateral. IORB (Interest on Reserve Balances) is the rate the Fed pays banks on reserves held at the central bank. Under normal conditions, SOFR trades close to IORB. A SOFR spike notably above IORB signals stress in short-term dollar funding markets — a warning sign worth watching well before it shows up in broader asset prices.

03 The Fed's Backstop

The Federal Reserve's Repo Market Backstop: Standing Repo Facility (SRF)

When banks use the Fed's emergency window, and how to position for intervention.

The Standing Repo Facility lets eligible institutions borrow cash overnight from the Fed against Treasury and agency collateral, at a pre-set rate, acting as a backstop against funding stress like that flagged in lesson 2. Elevated, sustained SRF usage is a direct signal that liquidity conditions in the repo market are tight enough that institutions are turning to the central bank's emergency facility rather than the open market.

04 Government Cash

The Government's Checking Account: Treasury General Account (TGA)

Tracking when government spending adds or drains liquidity from markets.

The TGA is the U.S. Treasury's operating account at the Fed. When the TGA balance rises (the Treasury issuing debt and building cash), money is effectively drained out of the private financial system into the government's account — a liquidity-tightening effect. When the TGA balance falls (the government spending down its cash), liquidity is released back into the system.

05 Crisis Signal

When Stocks and Bonds Fall Together: The "Doom Loop Detector"

Spotting Treasury market dysfunction before Fed intervention.

Stocks and bonds typically have a degree of negative correlation — when investors flee equities, they often rotate into the relative safety of government bonds. When stocks and bonds fall together sharply, it suggests something more serious: a broad liquidity event where investors are selling everything, including normally safe assets, often to raise cash. This pattern has historically preceded direct central bank intervention to restore market function.

06 Cross-Reference

The Dollar Index (DXY): A Key Gauge of Global Liquidity

Four critical DXY patterns that signal when to go long or short USD pairs.

Beyond its role as a currency-strength gauge (Module 13), a sharply rising DXY often signals tightening global dollar liquidity — since the dollar's global funding role (Module 13, lesson 1) means dollar strength can squeeze borrowers worldwide who owe dollar-denominated debt. A falling DXY, conversely, often accompanies looser, more abundant global liquidity conditions.

07 Confirmation Signals

Gold and Bitcoin: Your Liquidity Confirmation Signals

Six patterns that confirm whether to be aggressive or defensive with positions.

Both gold (Module 14, lesson 3) and, more recently, Bitcoin have been watched by some traders as liquidity barometers — both tend to perform well during periods of abundant, loose liquidity and central bank balance sheet expansion (next lesson), and tend to struggle when liquidity tightens sharply. Used alongside DXY and the funding-stress indicators above, they offer a useful cross-check rather than a standalone signal.

08 Structural Liquidity

How Central Bank Balance Sheets Shape Global Liquidity

How a balance sheet creates or destroys liquidity, and where to find the data.

This extends Module 05, lesson 5's introduction of QE and QT. When a central bank expands its balance sheet (QE — buying assets like government bonds), it injects new liquidity into the financial system. When it contracts its balance sheet (QT), it withdraws liquidity. Most major central banks publish their balance sheet size and composition on a regular basis, freely available to the public.

09 Application

How Central Bank Balance Sheets Changes Impact Forex

Why traders must understand balance sheet expansion and contraction to trade currency pairs.

Comparing the relative pace of balance sheet expansion or contraction between two central banks adds a further layer to the policy divergence framework from Module 05. A central bank expanding its balance sheet aggressively while another contracts can pressure the former's currency, independent of (and sometimes ahead of) any change in headline interest rates.

10 Long-Term View

Global M2 Money Supply: The Long-Term Liquidity Indicator

A leading liquidity indicator for longer-horizon trading decisions.

M2 measures a broad definition of money supply (cash, checking deposits, and easily-convertible near-money assets) across major economies. Tracking aggregated global M2 growth gives a longer-term, slower-moving read on liquidity conditions — complementary to the faster-moving, more tactical indicators covered earlier in this module (SOFR, TGA, balance sheets), useful for forming a longer-horizon view rather than day-to-day positioning.

Quiz: Global Liquidity

1. What does a SOFR spike notably above IORB typically signal?

Answer: B. SOFR trading notably above IORB indicates funding stress in overnight dollar lending markets.

2. What happens to market liquidity when the Treasury General Account (TGA) balance rises?

Answer: A. A rising TGA balance pulls cash out of the private financial system, tightening liquidity.

3. Why is stocks and bonds falling together considered a more serious signal than a typical equity selloff?

Answer: A. Since stocks and bonds typically show some negative correlation, both falling together can indicate broader market dysfunction.

4. Why might gold and Bitcoin be watched as liquidity confirmation signals?

Answer: A. Both assets have shown a tendency to track broader liquidity conditions, making them a useful cross-check alongside other indicators.

5. How can diverging central bank balance sheet trajectories affect a currency, independent of headline rate decisions?

Answer: A. Balance sheet expansion or contraction adds liquidity-driven pressure on a currency that can move independently of, and sometimes ahead of, rate decisions.
