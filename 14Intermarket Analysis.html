Module 14 of the Trading Curriculum

Intermarket Analysis

Currency prices don't move in isolation. When oil surges, certain currencies follow. When bond yields diverge, exchange rates adjust. When investors pile into stocks, safe-haven currencies usually weaken. Intermarket analysis connects the dots between the world's major asset classes.

01 Foundations

What Is Intermarket Analysis?

Reading stocks, bonds, commodities, and currencies together.

Intermarket analysis studies the relationships between major asset classes — equities, bonds, commodities, and currencies — to understand what's actually driving a move in any one of them. A currency move that looks confusing in isolation often makes immediate sense once you check what oil, gold, or bond yields were doing at the same time.

02 The Core Lens

Risk-On / Risk-Off: How Market Mood Moves Currencies

This framework, first introduced in Module 12, is the central organizing lens for everything in this module.

Equities rallying, commodity prices firm, and bond yields rising together typically describes a risk-on environment that lifts AUD, NZD, and commodity currencies. The opposite combination — falling equities, falling yields, a firmer dollar — describes risk-off conditions. Every relationship covered in this module connects back to this basic mood, even when the specific mechanism (oil, copper, bond spreads) differs.

04 Commodities

How Gold Affects AUD/USD and USD/CHF

Why Australia and Switzerland react differently to gold moves.

Australia is a major gold producer, so rising gold prices tend to support its terms of trade and, by extension, AUD — a positive correlation. Switzerland's relationship is different: CHF has historically carried safe-haven characteristics of its own (Module 07), so gold and CHF often strengthen together during the same risk-off episodes, without gold directly driving the Swiss economy the way it does Australia's exports.

06 Evolving Relationships

The Dollar and Oil: Why the Old Rules No Longer Apply

How the U.S. shale revolution changed the relationship.

Historically, the U.S. was a large net oil importer, so higher oil prices drained dollars abroad to pay for it — supporting an inverse dollar-oil relationship. The shale revolution turned the U.S. into a major producer and, at times, a net exporter, weakening that old inverse link considerably. The relationship today is far less mechanically reliable than older trading lore suggests.

Practical implication: don't assume textbook dollar-oil inverse correlations hold in current conditions — check the relationship empirically rather than relying on dated rules of thumb.
07 Commodities

Copper and Industrial Metals: What They Signal for Currencies

A read on global growth, and a signal for AUD/USD.

Copper is heavily used in construction and manufacturing, making it a widely watched proxy for global industrial demand — sometimes nicknamed "Dr. Copper" for its perceived diagnostic value on the health of the global economy. As a major metals exporter, Australia's currency tends to track copper (and broader industrial metals) prices, alongside its more direct gold-export link from lesson 4. China's demand, as the world's largest metals consumer, is a key swing factor behind copper's moves.

08 Commodities

Silver: The Metal That Can't Make Up Its Mind

A split personality between safe haven and industrial commodity.

Silver behaves partly like gold (a monetary/safe-haven metal) and partly like copper (an industrial input used heavily in electronics and solar panels) — meaning it can react inconsistently to the same macro news depending on which "personality" is dominant at the time. The gold-silver ratio (how many ounces of silver equal one ounce of gold) is a commonly watched gauge of which characteristic is currently in control.

09 Fixed Income

How Bond Yields Affect Currency Movements

Why higher yields attract foreign capital.

This directly extends Module 05, lesson 7: rising government bond yields tend to attract foreign capital seeking better returns, supporting the local currency, all else equal. Bond yields often move ahead of formal central bank decisions, making them a useful leading signal layered on top of the policy framework from Module 05.

10 Fixed Income

How Bond Yield Spreads Affect Currency Movements

Why carry trades amplify the effect.

The yield spread between two countries' government bonds — rather than either country's yield in isolation — is often the more direct driver of the exchange rate between their currencies, echoing the rate-differential logic from Module 10 (Carry Trade). A widening spread in favor of one country tends to support that currency; a narrowing spread tends to pressure it.

11 Fixed Income

How Fixed Income Securities Affect Currency Movements

Comparing government bond benchmarks across countries.

Traders typically compare specific maturity benchmarks across countries — the 2-year for near-term rate expectations, the 10-year for longer-term growth and inflation expectations — to build the kind of cross-country comparison this lesson and the previous one describe. Module 19 (Beginner's Guide to Global Liquidity) revisits related bond-market signals from a liquidity-stress angle.

12 Equities

Equities and Currencies: The Big Picture

How equities shape risk sentiment and drive major currency moves.

Strong, rising equity markets are typically associated with a risk-on mood (lesson 2) that benefits higher-beta currencies, while sharp equity selloffs tend to coincide with flight-to-safety flows into the dollar, yen, and franc. This relationship is explored in much greater depth, including the specific Nikkei/USD-JPY case study, in Module 15 (Using Equities to Trade FX).

Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Intermarket Analysis

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. Why does AUD/USD tend to track gold prices?

Answer: A. As a major gold exporter, Australia's terms of trade and currency tend to benefit when gold prices rise.

2. Why has the historical inverse dollar-oil relationship weakened in recent years?

Answer: A. Shifting from a net importer to a major producer changed the structural relationship between oil prices and dollar demand.

3. Why is silver sometimes described as having a "split personality"?

Answer: A. Silver's dual industrial and monetary uses can make it react inconsistently depending on which characteristic dominates at a given time.

4. What typically matters more for currency moves: a country's absolute bond yield, or its yield spread versus another country?

Answer: A. The yield spread, not either country's yield in isolation, is generally the more direct driver of the exchange rate between two currencies.

5. What is the broad risk-on/risk-off lens that connects most of this module's relationships?

Answer: A. The risk-on/risk-off mood is the unifying lens behind most of the specific intermarket relationships covered in this module.

★ Bonus Lesson

Beta Currencies: First to Rally, First to Crack

How AUD/JPY and NZD/JPY serve as real-time gauges of market mood.

"Beta currencies" move with amplified sensitivity to global risk sentiment — AUD and NZD against JPY are classic examples, combining a high-beta, risk-sensitive currency against a traditional low-yield funding/haven currency (Module 10). Because of this sensitivity, pairs like AUD/JPY and NZD/JPY are often watched by traders across all asset classes as a quick, real-time pulse check on whether the market is leaning risk-on or risk-off at any given moment — useful context to check even if you don't trade these pairs directly.
