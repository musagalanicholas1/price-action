Module 22 of the Trading Curriculum

Keeping a Trading Journal

Most traders blame their strategy when results don't improve. The real problem is almost always execution, and execution is invisible without a journal. This module shows you exactly how to build one that makes you better.

01 Foundations

Why Traders Need a Journal

Completely different from a broker trade log.

A broker's trade log shows what happened: entry, exit, profit or loss. A trading journal captures something a broker log never can — why you took the trade, what you were thinking, and whether you actually followed your plan (Module 19). The real reason most retail traders lose money is usually invisible without this layer: not a bad strategy, but inconsistent execution of a reasonable one.

02 Structure

The Three Layers Every Trading Journal Needs

The layer most beginners skip is the one that matters most.

LayerWhat it captures
QuantitativeEntry, exit, size, P&L, R-multiple — the numbers a broker log already gives you
ContextualSetup type, market environment (Module 03), confluence factors present (Module 01)
PsychologicalWhat you were thinking and feeling, whether you followed your plan exactly — most often skipped, most often where the real lessons live
03 Pre-Trade

Before You Trade: Planning Your Journal Entry

What to write before you place a single order.

  • Setup identified — which specific system rule (Module 21) this trade matches.
  • Trade signal — the exact condition that confirmed entry was valid.
  • Entry trigger — the precise price or candle close that initiated the trade.
  • Exit levels — stop-loss and target, defined before entry, not adjusted reactively afterward.
  • Management plan — any planned scaling (Module 25) or trailing-stop rule for this specific trade.
Why before, not during: decisions written down before entry are made with a clear head; decisions made once a position is open are contaminated by the emotional pull of unrealized profit or loss.
04 Post-Trade

After You Trade: The Retrospective

A structured review that goes beyond profit and loss.

A useful retrospective asks: did I follow my plan exactly, regardless of outcome? What actually happened versus what I expected? What is the one specific, actionable lesson from this trade — not a vague feeling, but something concrete enough to act on next time?

A critical distinction: a losing trade executed exactly per plan is a process success, even with a negative outcome. A winning trade that broke the plan is a process failure, even with a positive outcome. Reviewing outcome alone, rather than process, teaches the wrong lessons over time.
05 The Real Numbers

Does Your Trading System Actually Make Money?

Win rate alone tells you almost nothing. Expectancy reveals the truth.

Win rate ignores the size of wins versus losses — a system winning 30% of the time can still be highly profitable if winners are large enough relative to losers, while a system winning 70% of the time can still lose money if losers are disproportionately large.

A meaningful sample is also essential — Module 21's testing discussion applies directly here: drawing conclusions from a handful of trades risks mistaking normal variance for a real edge or a real flaw.

06 Review Cadence

How to Review Your Trading Journal

A four-level review cadence, and why you should only make one change per cycle.

CadenceFocus
DailyQuick check: did today's trades follow the plan?
WeeklySpot short-term patterns in execution or setup quality
MonthlyCalculate expectancy and other statistics (lesson 5) across a meaningful sample
QuarterlyConsider one deliberate, evidence-based rule change (echoing Module 19's 90-day cadence)

An error log — a running list of specific, recurring mistakes (entering too early, ignoring the higher timeframe, moving a stop reactively) — accelerates this process by making patterns visible across many entries at once rather than relying on memory.

07 Sustaining the Habit

Keep Your Trading Journal Alive With These 5 Habits

What keeps a journaling practice alive past the initial enthusiasm.

Habit 1

Journal immediately

Write the entry right after the trade, not from memory days later.

Habit 2

Keep the template short

A journal so long it feels like a chore gets abandoned within weeks.

Habit 3

Schedule the review

Treat the weekly/monthly review as a fixed appointment, not an optional extra.

Habit 4

Journal losses honestly

The instinct to skip documenting a bad trade is exactly when the data matters most.

Habit 5

Revisit old entries

Periodically reread entries from months ago — patterns often only become visible with that distance.

Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Keeping a Trade Journal

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. What does a trading journal capture that a broker's trade log cannot?

Answer: A. The psychological and contextual layers — reasoning and plan adherence — are what a journal adds beyond the raw broker log.

2. A trade loses money but was executed exactly according to plan. How should this be classified in review?

Answer: A. Reviewing process rather than outcome alone is essential — a plan-following loss is a process success.

3. Why can a system with only a 30% win rate still be highly profitable?

Answer: A. Expectancy accounts for both win rate and the relative size of wins versus losses, which is why win rate alone can be misleading.

4. Why does the review cadence framework recommend only one deliberate rule change per quarterly cycle?

Answer: A. Isolating one change at a time mirrors the same evidence-based principle from Module 19's 90-day plan evolution framework.

5. Why is journaling losing trades honestly especially important?

Answer: A. Avoiding documentation of losses removes exactly the data most likely to reveal patterns worth correcting.
★ Bonus Lesson

Which Trading Journal Tool Should You Use?

A practical guide to the main journaling platforms available today.

Options generally range from free, simple spreadsheet templates to dedicated journaling software with automated trade import, screenshot annotation, and built-in expectancy/statistics calculations. Costs and feature sets vary, and the right choice depends on how much automation versus manual reflection you want in your process.

The one rule that applies regardless of tool: the best journal is the one you actually use consistently. An elaborate, feature-rich tool abandoned after two weeks is worth less than a simple spreadsheet maintained for a year.
