Support and Resistance Levels

To start your education on technical analysis, let's begin with the basics: support and resistance — the foundation every other charting tool in this curriculum builds on top of.

01 Foundations

What Is Support and Resistance?

The most basic concept in price action analysis, and how to start plotting it.

Support is a price level where buying pressure has historically been strong enough to stop a decline and push price back up — think of it as a floor. Resistance is the opposite: a level where selling pressure has historically been strong enough to stop a rally and push price back down — a ceiling.

These levels exist because price isn't moved by some abstract force — it's moved by real participants placing real orders. At a support level, enough buyers have shown up before that traders expect them to show up again. At a resistance level, enough sellers have shown up before that traders expect the same. The level becomes somewhat self-reinforcing: because many traders are watching it, many traders act on it, which helps the level continue to "work."

How to Start Plotting Levels

  1. Look for price areas where the market has reversed direction more than once.
  2. Mark these as horizontal zones rather than single exact lines — price rarely reverses at the identical price twice.
  3. Note how many times each level has been tested — more tests generally means more traders are watching it, though as later lessons in this curriculum explore, a level tested too many times can also start to weaken.
The role-reversal principle: once resistance is convincingly broken, it often becomes new support — and once support is broken, it often becomes new resistance. The market's memory of a level doesn't disappear just because price has moved beyond it.
02 Charting Tools

Trend Lines

The most basic — but powerful — charting tool in the toolbox.

A trend line is simply a straight line connecting a series of swing highs or swing lows, drawn to capture the angle of the prevailing trend. In an uptrend, you connect a series of rising swing lows; in a downtrend, you connect a series of falling swing highs. The line itself becomes a dynamic, moving version of support or resistance — sloped rather than horizontal.

Drawing a Valid Trend Line

  • You need at least two touch points to draw a line, but a third touch is what actually starts to validate it as meaningful.
  • The line should touch the swing points themselves as closely as possible — forcing a line to fit by ignoring where price actually turned defeats its purpose.
  • A trend line that gets touched more times, over a longer period, with cleaner reactions each time, is generally considered more significant.
What a broken trend line means: a decisive close through a trend line is one of the earliest signals that the trend's angle of ascent or descent may be changing — though, as with horizontal support/resistance, it doesn't automatically mean a full reversal is underway.
03 Charting Tools

Trend Channels

Taking trend lines a step further to see opportunities within a trend.

A trend channel adds a second, parallel line on the opposite side of price from your original trend line — connecting the swing highs in an uptrend (above price) or the swing lows in a downtrend (below price). Together, the two lines form a sloped "tube" that price tends to oscillate within.

Trading Opportunities a Channel Reveals

Location in channelTypical interpretation
Bounce off the lower line (uptrend)Potential continuation entry, buying near support
Approach to the upper line (uptrend)Potential area to take profit or watch for resistance
A break beyond either channel lineA signal the trend's pace may be accelerating or stalling
04 Application

How to Trade Support and Resistance

Two simple ideas: the Bounce and the Break.

The Bounce

Trading the bounce means entering in the direction of the reversal once price reaches a support or resistance level and shows signs of rejecting it — buying near support expecting a move back up, or selling near resistance expecting a move back down.

The Break

Trading the break means entering in the direction of the move once price decisively closes beyond a level — buying once resistance breaks (expecting it to act as new support going forward), or selling once support breaks (expecting it to act as new resistance).

Both ideas reappear constantly in this curriculum: the bounce is the seed of the "Zone Reversal" setup and the break is the seed of the "Break-and-Retest" setup covered in much greater depth in Modules 01 and 02.
05 A Special Case

Psychological Levels

"Invisible lines" that act as support or resistance simply because so many traders are watching them.

Psychological levels are round numbers — 1.1000, 1.5000, 100.00 — that tend to attract attention and resting orders purely because humans naturally gravitate toward round figures, independent of any specific chart history at that exact price. Because so many traders place orders near these levels (take-profits, stop-losses, and fresh entries alike), the levels can behave like genuine support or resistance even without a prior structural reason.

This is the same concept introduced briefly in Module 02, lesson 2 of the broader curriculum (round numbers as key levels) — here at the foundational level, the takeaway is simply: don't dismiss a level just because nothing "special" happened there before. Sometimes the number itself is the reason.

06 Synthesis

Summary: Trading Support and Resistance

A quick recap, and how to spot potential opportunities with these zones.

  1. Support and resistance are price floors and ceilings created by real buying and selling pressure, not abstract lines.
  2. Trend lines connect swing points to capture a sloped, dynamic version of support or resistance.
  3. Trend channels add a second parallel line, framing the trend's full range of typical movement.
  4. The Bounce and the Break are the two foundational ways to trade any level — fading the rejection, or following the breakout.
  5. Psychological levels show that a level doesn't need prior chart history to matter — sometimes a round number is enough.

Everything in this grade is the seed of the more advanced price action framework in Module 01 of the main curriculum (Price Action Trading) — supply/demand zones are a more refined version of support/resistance, and the Big Three setups formalize the Bounce and the Break into precise, rules-based entries.

Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Support and Resistance Levels

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. Why do support and resistance levels tend to "work" as self-reinforcing zones?

Answer: A. Widely-watched levels attract orders from many participants, which itself helps reinforce the reaction at that price.

2. What happens, according to the role-reversal principle, once resistance is convincingly broken?

Answer: A. Broken resistance frequently flips into new support, and vice versa for broken support.

3. What is the key difference between a trend line and a trend channel?

Answer: A. The channel extends the single trend line into a full "tube" by adding a parallel line through the opposite swing points.

4. What is the core difference between trading "the Bounce" and trading "the Break"?

Answer: A. The Bounce fades a level's rejection, while the Break follows price once it closes decisively beyond the level.

5. Why can a round number like 1.5000 act as support or resistance even without any prior chart history there?

Answer: A. Psychological levels work because of human attention and order clustering around round figures, not because of prior price history at that exact level.
