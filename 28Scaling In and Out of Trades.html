Module 28 of the Trading Curriculum

Scaling In and Out of Trades

Opening a trade is the easy part. Knowing what to do while it's open is where most traders fall short. Learn how to scale in and out of positions: adding to winners, taking partial profits, and managing risk across multiple entries without ever violating your original risk budget.

01 Foundations

Scaling: What It Is, What It Isn't, and What It Costs

What every choice costs, including the ones most traders think are free.

Scaling means adding to or removing from a position while it's still open, rather than entering and exiting all at once. It's often confused with simply "changing your mind" mid-trade — the difference is that scaling follows a pre-planned structure (decided before entry, per Module 22's planning lesson), while changing your mind reactively is closer to abandoning the plan altogether.

ChoiceWhat it costs
All-at-once entryNo averaging benefit, but simplest to manage and journal
Scaling in (pyramiding)Requires careful total-risk tracking; can increase average cost basis if mistimed
Scaling outLocks in partial profit, but caps the size benefiting from a continued large move
Nothing here is free. Every scaling choice trades one benefit for another — there is no version of scaling that captures all the upside of holding full size with none of the downside.
02 Taking Profit

How to Scale Out of a Winning Position

When to take partial profits, and how to plan it before you ever open the trade.

A scale-out plan should specify, before entry: how much to close at each level, where those levels sit (typically prior structural levels from Module 01), and what happens to the remaining size — held to a final target, or managed with a trailing stop (Module 03).

03 Adding to Winners

Pyramiding: How to Add to a Winning Position

Adding to a winning position in decreasing sizes, without ever exceeding your original risk budget.

Pyramiding adds to a position as it moves favorably, typically at each new confirmed pullback in a trend (Module 01's trend continuation setup), with each additional entry sized smaller than the last — preserving the principle that total risk across all tranches never exceeds what a single full-size entry would have risked under Module 24's per-trade limit.

The discipline this demands: each new tranche needs its own valid setup confirmation (Module 01) — pyramiding isn't simply adding size because a trade is currently profitable, which is closer to chasing than to a structured system.
04 Sizing Each Tranche

ATR-Based Sizing for Scale-Ins and Scale-Outs

Anchoring every tranche to what the market is actually moving.

Extending Module 26's volatility-adjusted sizing to scaled trades: space each pyramid entry by a fraction of current ATR (rather than a fixed pip distance) so additions are appropriately spaced for current volatility, and size each tranche's stop using the same ATR-buffer principle from Module 27, lesson 4.

ElementATR-based approach
Spacing between pyramid entriesA set multiple of current ATR, not a fixed pip count
Each tranche's individual stopStructural level ± ATR buffer (Module 27)
Scale-out target spacingSet relative to ATR-derived measured moves (Module 02)
05 The Dangerous Variant

Scaling Into a Zone: Adding to a Losing Position

One of the most dangerous and most misunderstood things you can do in trading.

Adding to a position that's currently moving against you — distinct from pyramiding into a winner — is sometimes done deliberately within a pre-defined demand or supply zone (Module 01), with strict prerequisites: the additional entry must still be within the original zone the trade thesis was built on, the total combined risk must still respect Module 24's per-trade limit, and the practice must never be used reactively as an emotional response to an unplanned loss.

Why most traders should not attempt this without passing those prerequisites first: the undisciplined version of this — adding to a loser hoping for a reversal, with no zone logic or total-risk ceiling — is one of the most common ways an otherwise survivable loss turns into an account-ending one. The structured version and the undisciplined version look superficially similar but are fundamentally different practices.
06 Synthesis

The Scaling Playbook

Rules, platform mechanics, and considerations across markets and prop firms, in one reference.

  1. Decide your scaling plan (in, out, both, or neither) before entry — never improvised mid-trade.
  2. Track total combined risk across all tranches against your Module 24 per-trade limit at every stage.
  3. Use ATR-based spacing and stops for each tranche (lesson 4), not fixed pip distances.
  4. Pyramid only into confirmed new setups within an established trend — never simply because a trade is currently profitable.
  5. If scaling into a losing zone, confirm it's within the original thesis zone and never a reactive response to an unplanned loss.
  6. Prop firm note: many funded-account challenges (Module 30) impose their own rules on total open risk — confirm scaling plans comply with those specific terms before applying this playbook on a funded account.
Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Scaling In and Out

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. What distinguishes planned scaling from simply "changing your mind" mid-trade?

Answer: A. Genuine scaling is pre-planned; reactive mid-trade changes are a departure from the original plan.

2. In pyramiding, why does each additional tranche typically use a smaller size than the previous one?

Answer: A. Decreasing tranche sizes keep total risk bounded as the position grows.

3. Why use ATR-based spacing between pyramid entries instead of a fixed pip distance?

Answer: A. ATR-based spacing keeps entries appropriately distanced relative to current market movement, similar to the volatility-adjusted sizing from Module 26.

4. What separates the disciplined version of scaling into a losing position from the dangerous, undisciplined version?

Answer: A. The structured version follows pre-set zone logic and a risk ceiling; the dangerous version is an unplanned emotional reaction.

5. Why does the Scaling Playbook specifically note a consideration for prop firm accounts?

Answer: A. Funded account challenge terms often include their own total-risk constraints that scaling plans need to respect.
