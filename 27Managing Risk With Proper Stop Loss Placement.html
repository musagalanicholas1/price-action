Module 27 of the Trading Curriculum

Managing Risk With Proper Stop Loss Placement

Most traders know what a stop loss is. A lot of them don't place it correctly. Learn exactly where a stop belongs on a chart, how to size a position around it, why obvious levels keep getting hit early, and what actually happens when a stop executes in a live account.

01 Foundations

What Is a Stop Loss?

A predetermined exit, and what it costs you to skip this step.

A stop loss is a pre-set order that automatically closes a trade once price reaches a specified level, capping the loss on that trade at a known, predetermined amount. Trading without one means a single adverse move has no defined limit — directly undermining every risk percentage and position sizing calculation from Modules 24 and 26, which all assume a defined exit point exists.

02 The Core Principle

The Golden Rule of Stop Placement

A subtle mistake in stop placement can ruin an otherwise solid trade.

The golden rule: a stop loss belongs at the price level where your original trade idea is objectively proven wrong — not at an arbitrary distance, and not at the maximum amount you're willing to lose. Many beginners reverse this logic, deciding how much they're willing to lose first and placing the stop there regardless of where the chart actually invalidates the trade.

03 Method 1

How to Set a Stop Loss Based on the Chart

Using support, resistance, trendlines, and structure to find your exact invalidation point.

ReferenceWhere the stop goes
Supply/demand zone (Module 01)Just beyond the far edge of the zone
Swing high/low (Module 01)Just beyond the relevant swing point
Trendline (Module 03)Just beyond a confirmed trendline break
Break-and-retest level (Module 02)Just beyond the retested level, on the far side from entry

"Just beyond" matters — placing a stop exactly at a well-known level, rather than slightly past it, runs directly into the next lesson's problem.

04 Method 2

How to Set a Stop Loss Using Volatility

ATR helps place smarter stops and add the right buffer.

This extends Module 26, lesson 3 directly: rather than (or in addition to) a pure chart-based stop, add a buffer based on a fraction of current ATR beyond the structural level — ensuring the stop accounts for the market's current typical noise, not just the static chart level itself.

05 A Common Frustration

Why Your Stop Keeps Getting Hit (And How to Fix It)

Stops placed at obvious levels get hit more often than they should.

Obvious technical levels — round numbers, clean swing points everyone can see on the same chart — attract resting orders from many participants at once, including stop-hunting style liquidity grabs (Module 02, lesson 5). Price often briefly pierces these exact levels before snapping back in the original direction, catching stops placed precisely there.

The fix: place stops a calculated distance beyond the obvious level (using the ATR buffer from lesson 4), rather than directly at it — requiring a genuine, larger move to trigger the stop rather than a brief, common liquidity-grab spike.
06 Discipline

Stop Loss Mistakes, Rules, and Execution

Five common mistakes, three rules you can't break, and what happens when a stop executes.

Mistake

Moving the stop further away

Widening a stop after entry to avoid taking a loss defeats the entire purpose of having one.

Mistake

No stop at all

"Mental stops" routinely fail under the emotional pressure of a live, losing position.

Mistake

Placing it at round numbers

Exactly the obvious-level problem from lesson 5.

Mistake

Sizing first, stop second

Reverses the correct order from lesson 2 — the chart should determine the stop, not the desired position size.

Mistake

Ignoring volatility

Using the same fixed pip stop in calm and volatile conditions alike.

Three Rules You Can't Break

  1. Never move a stop further away from price once a trade is open.
  2. Always use a real, broker-side order — never rely on a mental stop alone.
  3. Always know your stop level before entering, never decide it after the fact.

When a stop executes in a live market, slippage (Module 06, lesson 5) can mean the actual fill differs slightly from the set level, especially during fast moves or low liquidity — a real-world detail your position sizing should leave reasonable room for, rather than assuming a perfect fill every time.

07 Synthesis

The Stop Loss Playbook

The complete framework in one lesson: a pre-trade checklist.

  1. Identify the chart level where your trade idea is objectively invalidated (lesson 2–3).
  2. Add an ATR-based buffer beyond that level to avoid common liquidity-grab spikes (lesson 4–5).
  3. Calculate position size from this final stop distance (Module 26) — never the reverse.
  4. Place a real, broker-side stop order immediately upon entry.
  5. Never move it further away once the trade is open.
Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Proper Stop Loss Placement

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. According to the golden rule, where should a stop loss actually be placed?

Answer: A. The stop should reflect chart invalidation, not an arbitrary dollar amount decided independently of the chart.

2. Why do stops placed exactly at obvious round numbers or swing points get hit more often than expected?

Answer: A. Crowded, well-known levels are more prone to brief stop-hunting spikes before the broader move resumes.

3. What is the correct order of operations between setting a stop and calculating position size?

Answer: A. The stop comes from chart invalidation; position size is then derived from that stop distance and the risk percentage (Module 26).

4. Why is a "mental stop" (no real broker-side order) considered risky?

Answer: A. Without an actual order in place, emotional pressure during a live losing trade often leads to the stop being ignored or moved.

5. Which of the three unbreakable rules in this module directly addresses the temptation to avoid taking a loss?

Answer: A. Moving a stop further away to avoid a loss is one of the most common and damaging stop-loss mistakes covered in this module.
