Position Sizing for Account Survival

Entries get the attention. Position sizing determines who survives. This module shows you the exact formula for calculating the right trade size for any pair and account currency, how to adjust for volatility, and how to make sure one bad session never takes you out of the game.

01 Foundations

What is Position Sizing?

What you're actually solving for, and why getting it wrong is the fastest way to blow an account.

Position sizing answers one specific question: given my risk percentage (Module 24) and my stop-loss distance, exactly how many units of this instrument should I trade? It is the bridge between an abstract risk rule ("I risk 1% per trade") and an actual, executable order size. Skipping this calculation — trading a habitual lot size instead — means your real dollar risk on any given trade is essentially random, disconnected from the risk percentage you intended.

02 The Formula

Calculating Your Position Size

A simple three-step formula for any currency pair and account currency.

A Worked Example

InputValue
Account balance$10,000
Risk per trade1% → $100
Stop distance50 pips
Pip value (standard lot, this pair)$10/pip
Position size$100 ÷ (50 × $10) = 0.2 standard lots
Pip value varies by pair and account currency. Always confirm the actual pip value for your specific pair and account denomination — most trading platforms display this directly, or it can be calculated from the pair's quote currency exchange rate.
03 A Smarter Approach

Volatility-Adjusted Position Sizing

A fixed pip stop treats every market condition the same — and that's a problem.

A 30-pip stop might be wide during a quiet, low-volatility period and uncomfortably tight during a volatile one — the same fixed pip distance means a very different probability of simply being "noise-stopped" depending on current conditions. Anchoring the stop distance to ATR (Module 02) rather than a fixed pip count adjusts automatically as volatility changes.

The key insight: as stop distance (the denominator in the sizing formula) changes with volatility, position size automatically adjusts in the opposite direction to keep dollar risk constant — wider stops in volatile conditions are offset by a smaller position, and vice versa.

04 Total Exposure

Portfolio Heat: Managing Your Total Risk Exposure

Sizing individual trades correctly is only half the job.

"Portfolio heat" refers to the total risk currently exposed across all open positions simultaneously. Even if each individual trade is sized correctly per Module 24's per-trade limit, having many positions open at once — especially correlated ones (Module 17) — can mean your real combined exposure is far larger than any single trade suggests.

Managing portfolio heat means setting a maximum total risk percentage across all open positions at once (echoing the layered risk budgeting from Module 24, lesson 4), and accounting for correlation when several open trades effectively express the same underlying view.

Q Check Your Understanding

Quiz: Position Sizing

Click an answer to reveal the explanation.

1. What specific question does position sizing answer?

Answer: A. Position sizing converts an abstract risk percentage and stop distance into an actual, executable trade size.

2. With a $100 dollar risk, a 50-pip stop, and a $10/pip value, what is the correct position size?

Answer: A. $100 ÷ (50 pips × $10/pip) = 0.2 standard lots.

3. Why does ATR-based, volatility-adjusted sizing work better than a fixed pip stop across changing conditions?

Answer: A. Tying stop distance to ATR keeps the resulting dollar risk consistent as volatility changes, unlike a fixed pip distance.

4. What does "portfolio heat" refer to?

Answer: A. Portfolio heat captures combined exposure across multiple open positions, which can exceed what any single per-trade limit suggests.

5. Why can three individually well-sized 1%-risk trades still create a risk management problem?

Answer: A. Correlated positions (Module 17) can stack risk rather than diversify it, meaning combined exposure can exceed what per-trade sizing alone suggests.
